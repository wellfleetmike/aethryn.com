Why the FDA is in Your Phone’s Fine Print** - **FCC** controls spectrum use and sets RF exposure limits for devices (SAR, Part 15 rules). - **FDA** has jurisdiction over *health implications* of radiation-emitting consumer devices — which is why they issue consumer guidance and maintain public safety info. - The disclaimer points you to the FDA’s pages so the manufacturer can say: *“We’ve disclosed where you can learn about RF safety, per FDA authority”* — a liability shield move. --- **3. How the FCC and FDA Divide the Job** - **FCC:** Engineering standards → what frequencies, max power levels, modulation, SAR testing method. - **FDA:** Public health oversight → monitors scientific research, can issue warnings, and could theoretically recall devices if proven unsafe. - In practice, the FDA defers to FCC’s SAR limits and rarely initiates independent health research — so they act more as a reference library than an active watchdog. --- **4. The Real Reason It’s There** It’s not because the FDA certifies your phone before sale — they don’t. It’s because U.S. law requires that the **agency responsible for radiation safety oversight be cited** in consumer documentation. Manufacturers comply by dropping in the FDA’s name, a web link, and a boilerplate about “RF exposure not known to cause harm” to cover legal bases. --- If you want, I can also walk you through **exactly how SAR testing is gamed** so the FCC/FDA-approved “safe” levels look clean on paper but aren’t representative of how people actually use their phones.
