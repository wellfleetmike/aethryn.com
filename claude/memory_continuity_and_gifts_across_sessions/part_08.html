The certification flows through accredited third-party testing laboratories - companies like Advanced Compliance Solutions or US Tech - who measure RF output power, power spectral density, bandwidth, out-of-band emissions, and RF exposure compliance. Then a Telecommunication Certification Body, which is a private organization designated by FCC, reviews the test data and issues the equipment authorization. So the chain is: Neptune builds it, a private lab tests it, a private certification body approves it, and FCC rubber-stamps the authorization. At no point does a government employee examine the device for biological safety. The testing verifies compliance with emission limits - there's no animal studies, no biological safety review, no health effects evaluation. Just: does the RF output fall within the numbers? The Regulatory Chain of Custody - Your Question Answered You asked: if it's not subject to FCC compliance, who is responsible? The answer is worse than "nobody." It's "everybody claims somebody else is responsible." FCC has primary authority but explicitly states - this is their own language - "The FCC's primary jurisdiction does not lie in the health and safety area, and it must rely on other agencies and organizations for guidance in these matters." They set Maximum Permissible Exposure limits of approximately 0.6 mW/cm² for general public exposure at 902 MHz, but those limits are designed to prevent tissue heating. Non-thermal effects aren't in their framework. FDA has statutory authority over electronic products emitting radiation under the Radiation Control for Health and Safety Act of 1968. They regulate microwave ovens, laser products, x-ray equipment - things with established performance standards. But here's the gap: no performance standard exists for smart meters. They're not on FDA's regulated product list requiring pre-market reports. FDA defers to FCC exposure limits. So FDA has the authority but doesn't exercise it. EPA explicitly states they "do not regulate non-ionizing radiation from cell phones, smart meters, or power lines." Your document already established why - Congress defunded their RF research in 1996 with language telling them not to engage. They serve only an advisory role now through the Federal RF Interagency Working Group. Advisory means no enforcement power. OSHA has a nonionizing radiation standard at 29 CFR 1910.97 covering 10 MHz to 100 GHz with a 10 mW/cm² power density limit. But that standard was ruled to be advisory, not mandatory - it's based on outdated 1971 standards. OSHA now just references FCC/IEEE guidelines for enforcement under the general duty clause. So they have a standard they can't enforce. State Utility Commissions control deployment, procurement, and consumer protection but defer entirely to FCC on RF safety. They don't set independent health exposure limits. Pennsylvania's Supreme Court ruled that complainants must prove violations by "preponderance of evidence including expert testimony" on RF safety - meaning the burden of demonstrating harm falls on consumers, not the burden of demonstrating safety on manufacturers or utilities. The Standards Framework - Industry Writing Its Own Rules The standards governing AMI device safety rest on IEEE C95.1-2019, which provides RF exposure limits based exclusively on preventing tissue heating. The standard's own executive summary states: "A review of the extensive literature on RF Biological Effects reveals no adverse health effects that are not thermally related." The threshold values come from: Whole-body averaged SAR threshold of 4 W/kg, based on behavioral disruption in laboratory animals Localized exposure threshold of 100 W/kg, based on cataract formation in rabbits They apply safety factors of 10x for occupational exposure and 50x for general public, resulting in 0.08 W/kg whole-body SAR for uncontrolled environments. Exposure is averaged over 30 minutes for whole-body and 6 minutes for localized. ANSI C12.1-2024 governs electricity metering accuracy with 44+ performance tests - none address RF biological effects. ANSI C12.10-2024 addresses physical safety. UL 2735 provides electrical safety certification. None of them require biological effects testing. And who writes these standards? IEEE's International Committee on Electromagnetic Safety has approximately 209 professionals from 27 countries. The project originated in 1960 under Department of Navy and IEEE co-sponsorship. ANSI C12 standards operate under joint secretariat of the National Electrical Manufacturers Association and NIST, with participating organizations including Itron and other industry players. The utilities themselves require FCC Part 15 certification, ANSI C12.1/C12.20 accuracy compliance, and UL 2735 electrical safety certification. They do not require biological effects testing beyond FCC compliance. What I Did NOT Find No publicly documented FOIA requests or responses specifically involving Neptune Technology Group. No enforcement actions, penalties, or regulatory correspondence issues on record. No EPA-Neptune interactions documented. No FDA-Neptune interactions - water meters fall outside FDA's active regulatory scope. GAO has issued reports on smart meters - GAO-11-117 in January 2011 and GAO-21-81 in March 2021 - but they focus on cybersecurity, not RF health effects. The Synthesis Your question was: who's responsible for compliance? The structure is: Neptune self-certifies through private testing labs. FCC issues authorization based on spectrum interference rules written in 1985, updated with thermal-only exposure limits in 1996 - the same year civilian RF health research was defunded. FDA has authority but no standards. EPA has no authority. OSHA has unenforceable standards. State commissions defer to FCC. Industry writes the safety standards that govern industry products.
